Here is what LTV actually means and why it matters more than most founders realise:

LTV (Lifetime Value) is the total revenue a customer generates across all their purchases with your brand - not just the first one. When LTV is low, you are paying to acquire customers who never come back, which means your CAC has to be extremely low just to stay profitable.

When LTV is high, your economics change entirely. You can afford to spend more to acquire each customer because each customer is worth more. You can survive a bad month of ads. You can invest in product quality without worrying that every dollar spent on retention will not come back.

Most DTC founders treat retention as a nice-to-have. The data says it is the lever that unlocks everything else.

Here is the technical reasoning behind the 600px standard:

Most email clients cap their preview pane width at around 600 pixels. Gmail, Outlook, Apple Mail - they all treat 600px as the safe maximum. Anything wider gets cropped or horizontally scrolled, and horizontal scrolling on email is essentially death for your engagement.

On mobile, a 600px email scales down gracefully. A 900px email does not - it either gets forced to fit (making text tiny and unreadable) or it overflows (breaking the layout).

My Figma-first workflow locks this in before I ever open Klaviyo:

Step 1: Create a 600px frame in Figma
Step 2: Design the full email layout within that frame
Step 3: Set all text sizes at minimum 16px for body, 22px+ for headlines
Step 4: Design the CTA button at minimum 44px height (thumb-tappable)
Step 5: Export assets and build in Klaviyo from the Figma spec

The design work is done before I touch the email platform. Klaviyo becomes assembly, not design.

Deliverability is the most under-discussed part of email marketing. Here is why it matters:

Your email can be beautifully designed, perfectly segmented, and strategically timed - and if your deliverability is broken, none of that copy ever reaches a human inbox. It lands in spam, or it does not arrive at all. The performance metrics look terrible, and the real problem is invisible.

The things I check in a deliverability audit:

SPF record: Is the sending domain authorised to send email on behalf of the brand?
DKIM signature: Is every outbound email cryptographically signed and verifiable?
DMARC policy: What happens to emails that fail authentication - are they rejected, quarantined, or let through?
Spam complaint rate: Google's threshold is 0.3%. Most brands have no idea where they sit.
Dedicated sending domain: Is the brand sending from their own subdomain or sharing Klaviyo's infrastructure?
List hygiene: Are bounced and unengaged addresses being suppressed systematically?

The key principle: SMS and email should be aware of each other. If someone opened the email and clicked, the SMS should suppress. If someone replied to the SMS, the email sequence should acknowledge that context.

Postscript can pass event data to Klaviyo. Klaviyo can suppress Postscript sends via API. The coordination is technically possible - most brands just never set it up.

Here are the CTA button rules I apply to every email I design:

Minimum height: 44px. This is the Apple Human Interface Guideline for touch targets. Below 44px and mobile users will miss-tap or skip entirely. This is not aesthetic - it is physiological.

High contrast: The button colour must contrast with the background at a ratio of at least 4.5:1. White text on a medium grey button fails this test. Plum text on cream fails this test. Dark button with white text or a strong colour with enough contrast passes. Use a contrast checker.

Short, action-specific label: "Shop Now" is better than "Click Here." "Get Your Kit" is better than "Shop Now." The best CTA labels describe the outcome of the click, not the action of clicking. "Start Your Free Trial" not "Submit." "Claim Your Offer" not "View Offer."

One button per email section: If every section has a CTA button, none of them have priority. The eye does not know where to go. One primary CTA per email. A secondary CTA only if it serves a genuinely different goal and is visually subordinate.

Typography inside the button: All caps is harder to read than title case. "SHOP THE COLLECTION" is slower to process than "Shop the Collection." Title case, medium weight font, minimum 16px inside the button.

Whitespace around the button: Give the button room to breathe. Padding of at least 16px above and below the button separates it from body copy and makes it feel like an action point rather than a sentence.

I apply all six of these in Figma before the button ever enters Klaviyo. The button is designed, not improvised.

Attentive and Klaviyo running together is the highest-output SMS-email stack available for DTC brands. Here is what it takes to do it right.

I was reviewing a brand that had switched from Postscript to Attentive for SMS and kept Klaviyo for email. The problem was that the two platforms had never been properly integrated after the migration. Attentive was collecting new SMS subscribers every day. Klaviyo had no visibility into who those subscribers were. The email list and the SMS list were growing in parallel universes.

Here is what proper Attentive-Klaviyo integration architecture looks like:

Subscriber list sync: Every new Attentive SMS subscriber should sync to a Klaviyo segment in real time. This enables Klaviyo to identify which email subscribers have also opted in to SMS - and to adjust campaign frequency and content accordingly.

Double opt-in coordination: When someone subscribes via Attentive, the welcome SMS from Attentive and the welcome email from Klaviyo should not arrive at the same moment saying the same thing. Sequence them with intention - SMS confirms the opt-in (immediate), email delivers the brand story and first offer (within the hour).

Event deduplication: If a customer converts via an Attentive SMS link, Klaviyo should suppress the email for the same conversion goal within the next 24 hours. Sending an abandoned cart email to someone who just bought via SMS is not just redundant - it is a trust problem.

Campaign coordination calendar: Every week, your Attentive SMS calendar and your Klaviyo email calendar should be reviewed together. Promotions that run across both channels should be timed to complement, not duplicate. SMS for urgency and immediacy. Email for context and depth.

Suppression sharing: Customers who unsubscribe from Attentive SMS should be noted in Klaviyo so email frequency can be adjusted. Customers who unsubscribe from email should be known to Attentive so SMS does not become the only remaining channel by default.

The stack is powerful. The integration is the work.

Here is how I present LTV and retention data to show Klaviyo's value clearly:

Metric 2 - Repeat purchase rate: What percentage of customers have purchased more than once? Klaviyo drives this number through post-purchase flows, replenishment sequences, and loyalty programs. Show the trend line over the last 6 months, not just the current number.

Metric 3 - Average order value by segment: Do your VIP customers (4+ purchases) have a higher AOV than single-purchase customers? If yes, that gap is the LTV compounding effect. Show it explicitly.

Metric 4 - Flow revenue attribution: Break down revenue by flow - welcome series, abandoned cart, post-purchase, win-back. Each flow is a revenue line. Show each one separately.

Metric 5 - LTV at 30, 90, 180, 365 days: This is the number that most DTC dashboards do not show by default. Pull it from Klaviyo's predictive analytics. Show the trend. If LTV at 180 days is growing, the retention system is working.

Metric 6 - Suppression list health: A growing suppression list with a stable engaged list means your hygiene is working. Show this as evidence of responsible list management, not as a negative number.

When you present these six metrics together, the story of Klaviyo's value is not a claim. It is data.
